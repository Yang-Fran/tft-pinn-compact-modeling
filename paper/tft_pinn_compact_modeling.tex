\begin{abstract}
This work presents a compact-model-centered physics-informed neural network (PINN) workflow for indium-gallium-zinc-oxide thin-film transistor (IGZO TFT) SPICE modeling with limited channel lengths. A compact model implemented in Python and Verilog-A establishes the measured-device baseline. A parameter PINN reduces the \(80/10~\mu\mathrm{m}\) holdout error from \(1.54\) to \(0.48\) decade by learning length-dependent compact parameters. A residual PINN identifies structured measured-device error, but over-corrects unseen lengths. These results support PINNs as parameter-extraction and diagnostic aids around a SPICE-compatible IGZO TFT model.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
IGZO thin-film transistor, compact model, SPICE, physics-informed neural network, residual modeling, Verilog-A
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
Compact models connect device characterization to circuit simulation and remain central to TFT-based circuit design \cite{lu2018review,nagel1975spice2}. IGZO TFT datasets are often limited to a few geometries and bias sweeps. Independent fits can reproduce measured curves, but circuit design needs a SPICE-compatible description useful across geometry and bias.

Conventional global compact fitting may fail to extrapolate reliably when only a few channel lengths are measured. Black-box neural current models offer flexible bias-dependent responses, but are not naturally SPICE-compatible and provide limited guidance for improving the underlying compact expression. We therefore retain the compact model as the simulation layer and use neural networks to learn length-dependent parameters and diagnose residual error.

We evaluate three measured IGZO TFTs, \(80/10\), \(80/20\), and \(80/40~\mu\mathrm{m}\), using neural models guided by compact-model structure \cite{raissi2019physics}. The contributions are a numerically consistent Python/Verilog-A compact model, holdout experiments separating fitting from length extrapolation, and parameter/residual PINN modules for parameter mapping and diagnosis.

\section{Modeling Workflow}
The drain current uses a custom, Level-1-inspired empirical IGZO TFT compact model rather than a standardized MOSFET level. For \(V_{DS}\geq0\),
\begin{equation}
 V_{\mathrm{eff}}=nV_T\ln\!\left[1+\exp\!\left(\frac{V_{GS}-V_{\mathrm{TH}}}{nV_T}\right)\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 I_D=\left[I_{\mathrm{OFF}}+\beta\frac{W}{L}V_{\mathrm{eff}}^m
 \left(1-e^{-|V_{DS}|/V_{DSAT}}\right)\right]
 \left(1+\lambda|V_{DS}|\right),
\end{equation}
where \(V_{DSAT}=V_{\mathrm{SAT0}}V_{\mathrm{eff}}/(V_{\mathrm{SAT0}}+V_{\mathrm{eff}})\). In the global fit, \(V_{\mathrm{TH}}\), \(\log\beta\), and \(\lambda\) vary affinely with \(1/L\), \(\log I_{\mathrm{OFF}}\) varies with \(\log(W/L)\), and other parameters are shared. The same equations are used in Python and Verilog-A/ngspice \cite{ngspice_manual}.

All measured devices have \(W=80~\mu\mathrm{m}\), with \(L=10\), \(20\), and \(40~\mu\mathrm{m}\). After averaging repeated sweeps, the dataset contains 2525 points: 404 output-curve points per device and 505, 404, and 404 transfer-curve points, respectively. The \(80/10\) holdout uses 1616 training and 909 test points; the other holdouts use 1717 and 808. Both curve types are present in every split. The loss is evaluated in log-current space,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{I} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i
    \left[\log_{10}(|I_{D,i}^{\mathrm{fit}}|+I_0)
    - \log_{10}(|I_{D,i}^{\mathrm{meas}}|+I_0)\right]^2,
\end{equation}
where \(I_0\) is a small numerical floor.
Networks are trained with the squared log-current loss in (3), whereas Table~\ref{tab:holdout} reports mean absolute log-current error in decades.

Because \(W\) is constant, the parameter PINN maps \((\log_{10}L-\log_{10}20)/0.35\) to seven bounded compact parameters. It uses one four-neuron Tanh layer, 8000 AdamW epochs, and 300 L-BFGS steps. Log-current, linear-current, weight-decay, and length-smoothness loss weights are \(1\), \(0.04\), \(0.001\), and \(0.02\); the best of seeds 3, 17, and 41 is retained. The residual PINN learns
\begin{equation}
    \log_{10} I_D^{\mathrm{res}} =
    \log_{10} I_D^{\mathrm{base}} +
    r_{\theta}(V_G,V_D,\log L).
\end{equation}
The residual model uses two 12-neuron Tanh layers, normalized \((V_G,V_D,\log L)\), 6000 AdamW epochs, 200 L-BFGS steps, and the same seeds.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Measured-device compact fitting}
Independent fitting gives mean absolute log errors of \(0.06\)--\(0.19\) decade. Python and Verilog-A/ngspice agree below \(10^{-3}\) decade on average, confirming simulator transferability.

\subsection{Traditional channel-length extrapolation}
A global conventional fit gives a \(1.54\)-decade holdout error for \(80/10~\mu\mathrm{m}\), while the other lengths remain below \(0.27\) decade. The shortest channel lies at the data boundary, where \(1/L\) scaling is least constrained. Enlarging the subthreshold-factor range improves fitting; explicit source/drain resistance gives no consistent gain.

\subsection{Parameter PINN for compact parameters}
Table~\ref{tab:holdout} averages output and transfer errors. The PINN reduces the poorly constrained \(80/10~\mu\mathrm{m}\) boundary holdout to \(0.48\) decade, while conventional scaling remains competitive for the other lengths. Thus, neural parameterization helps the difficult boundary case but is not universally superior.

\subsection{Residual PINN for diagnosis}
The residual PINN is evaluated separately as a diagnostic and local-correction layer on top of the parameter-PINN baseline. On measured devices it reduces curve-level mean log error by \(32.5\%\)--\(81.9\%\), confirming that structured bias-dependent residuals remain after parameter mapping. However, true held-out-length tests worsen for all six device/curve combinations. The correction magnitude rises to \(0.30\)--\(0.73\) decade on unseen lengths, showing that the learned residual patterns do not extrapolate reliably.

Bias-region analysis gives the clearest interpretation. On measured devices, the largest corrections occur in low-\(V_G\)/high-\(V_D\), high-\(V_G\)/low-\(V_D\), and mid-\(V_G\)/low-\(V_D\) regions, suggesting missing descriptions in low-current transfer behavior and high-field operation. Their failure to transfer across \(L\) means these patterns are candidate compact-model deficiencies rather than established physical mechanisms. The residual network is therefore useful for locating where the compact expression should be refined, but not as an extrapolative SPICE layer.

\section{Conclusion}
The compact-model-centered workflow preserves SPICE compatibility while improving the shortest-channel holdout through length-dependent PINN parameters. Residual patterns expose candidate compact-model deficiencies but do not extrapolate reliably. Future work will add width variation and translate robust residual patterns into explicit model terms. Data and paper-generation files are available at \url{https://github.com/Yang-Fran/tft-pinn-compact-modeling}.
